# Financial Statements
## Cash Accounting vs Accrual Accounting
When recording changes in a business's income statement:

Cash accounting refers to logging changes in Revenue, Expenses & Profit **when cash is received / given**. As a result, revenue and expenses for services are often logged *asynchronously*, leading to inconsistent profit. For example,
* If I receive cash in advance for a service next month, I will still log that cash as Revenue for *this month*, leaving my expenses blank. I will then log my expenses for the service the next month, when I deliver it. This inflates the profits for this month.
* If I deliver the service this month and receive cash the next month, this month will reflect only the expenses, while next month will reflect the payment as revenue. This deflates the profits for this month.

Accrual Accounting logs Revenue, Expenses and Profits **all together, regardless of asynchronous cash payment**. As a result, the statements reflect a more consistent profit which is closer to reality.
* If I receive payment this month, in advance for a service next month, I will record it as *Deferred Revenue* this month, and as revenue *next month*. It will be in sync with the expenses of the service next month.
* If I deliver a service this month, for payment next month, I record the payment as *Accounts Receivable*, and as revenue *this month*. It is in sync with the expenses of the service, which are also this month.

## Balance sheet & Income statement
The balance sheet over a time period (e.g. month 1) is a *snapshot* of the assets & liabilities of a company. A very simple balance sheet is given:
<u>**Balance sheet (Month 1)**</u>
* <span style="color:#00ff00">Cash</span>: 100
* <span style="color:#00ff00">Accounts Receivable</span>: 400
* <span style="color:red">Deferred Revenue</span>: 0
Equity = 500

The income statement, on the other hand, shows the Revenue, Expenses and Income for a specific month, which would change the *Cash* on hand, thus affecting that month's balance sheet
<u>**Income statement (Month 2)**</u>
* <span style="color:#00ff00">Revenue</span>: 400
* <span style="color:red">Expenses</span>: 200
* <span style="color:">Income</span>: 200

## Cash Flow Statement
We use another statement to track the changes in the amount of Cash that our business has. While Income is the main contributor to Cash, changes in Deferred Revenue and Accounts Receivable also affect it. We can use the cash flow statement to track these three contributors.
<u>**Cash Flow (Month 1)**</u>
* **Starting Cash**: 100
* Net Income: 200
* Accounts Receivable: -400 (Accounts receivable is money that we could have had this month, but we'll get next month. It is thus a loss)
* Deferred Revenue: 0
* **Final Cash**: -100

The cash flow statement reconciles the changes in cash as seen in every balance sheet

# Price-Earnings Ratio
* Calculation: (Mkt price per share) $\div$ (Company earnings per Share)
* Note: EPS is generally in 2 forms: 
    * Trailing EPS: Current Share volume, Earnings over the past 12 months $\rightarrow$ Calculates **Trailing P/E**
    * Forward EPS: Current Share volume, Future projected Earnings $\rightarrow$ Calculates **Forward P/E**
* Use in valuation: 
    * "The P/E ratio indicates the dollar amount an investor can expect to invest in a company to receive \$1 of that company’s earnings. It’s sometimes called the price multiple because it shows how much investors are willing to pay per dollar of earnings. Investors are paying \$20 for $1 of current earnings if a company trades at a P/E multiple of 20x."
    * Can use comparitively, between company and industry. If a company is consistently below the industry standard P/E, this could mean it is undervalued in comparison to the industry
* Limitations:
    * Trailing P/E doesn't tell us much about the future performance of the company.
    * Forward P/E depends on who is projecting earnings; may be under / over estimated
* The *PEG ratio*
    * P/E compares stock price to earnings. But it doesn't account for the *growth in earnings*
    * **Price/earnings-to-growth** ratio is calculated by: (Trailing P/E) $\div$ (Earnings Growth Rate)
    * This can give us more info than the P/E, particularly in telling us if a stock if under / over valued

# Price-Book Ratio
* Calculation: (Mkt Price per share) $\div$ (Book Value Per Share)
* Book Value: Total assets - intangible assets - total liabilities
* Use in valuation:
    * P/B can tell us how much value customers attach to a company's equity compared to the book value of that equity
    * Can be used to tell if a stock is over / under valued
* Limitations:
    * Since the Book value uses tangible assets, it doesn't accurately reflect company value in the case that is has an intangible asset with a high value, such as brand name
    * Since accounting standards may vary across firms, the measure of book value may not consistently record value
    * However, in times of negative earnings, the P/B ratio still provides a measure of value while P/E is useless

# Return On Equity
* Definition: A ratio measuring how effectively a company uses shareholders’ equity to generate net income
* Calculation: $ROE = \frac{Net\ Income}{Shareholder's\ Equity}$

* Shareholder's Equity: *Total Assets* - *Total Debt*. It is the company's net worth, the **dollar amount that would be returned to shareholders if the company were to be liquidated*
    * Difference with Book Value? Book Value only includes *Tangible Assets*, while Shoreholder's Equity includes *Intangible Assets*

* Net income: *Net Revenue* - *All Expenses*

* Use in valuation: 
    * Used as a guage of *profitability* 
    * Can be used to estimate future growth rate of the company: Given by $ROE \cdot Retention\ Ratio$
        * Retention Ratio is the fraction of Net Income that is reinvested into the company.
        * Esseentially, this value tells us *Profitability* $\cdot$ *Share of Profit that is reinvested* $\Rightarrow$ growth rate

# Gross Margin
* Definition: Gross margin is the percentage of a company's revenue retained after subtracting COGS: Cost of Goods Sold. Measures the profitability of manufacturing activities
    * COGS is the **direct costs associated with producing goods**, such as material & labour costs
* Calculation: $Gross\ Margin = (\frac{Revenue\ -\ COGS}{Revenue})\cdot 100$
* Net Margin: Percentage of revenue retained after COGS *and* other business expenses, i.e. taxes, distribution, etc.
