## Preferred VS Common Stocks
Owners of preferred stocks have a higher claim over distributions than commons stock holders. They trade this for their voting rights in corporate governance

**Distributions** refer to dividends and returning money in case of liquidation. Preferred stock holders have priority in receiving these.

# Derivatives
From *Hull*: "A derivative can be defined as a financial instrument whose value depends on (or derives from) the values of other, more basic, underlying variables"

## Forward & Spot contracts
Forward contract is a conteract to exchange an asset for an agreed upon price at a specified future date. In contrast, Spot contract is an exchange which happens immediately. 

### Long vs Short Positions
There are 2 positions in a trade: A *long* and *short* position.
At the time of the trade, the party *purchasing the asset* is in a long position, while the party *selling the asset* is in a short position

* **Long** - Buying with the expectation of price going up
* **Short** - Selling with the expectation of the price going down

So in a forward contract, the party buying the asset is in a long position, the seller is in the short position.

#### Note:
The derivative here is not the underlying asset that will be traded at the time of maturity of the contract. The derivative **is the contract**. While the terms of the contract are the same, e.g. 100 kilos of mangos will be sold at a certain date at a certain price, the asset here being traded *is the forward contract*. The contract has a price of its own. This depends on market forces.

## Futures

Futures are just like forward contracts. Forward contracts are traded over the counter, while Futures are traded on exchanges.

Practically, most traders buy and sell a future before the contract matures, so they don't have to deal with the underlying asset. 

In case they hold to maturity, they would take over the role of the buyer, i.e. the long position. They would receive the asset, e.g. some commodity, or cash.

## Options
Options are similar to futures: they are contracts for a future exchange, can be traded on markets. **Main difference** is: options allow you the *right* to fulfill the contract at point of maturity, but you are *not obliged to*. You can stop the contract from executing.

* Call Option: Owner of a call option is in the long position - they have the right to buy the underlying asset
* Put option: Short position - holder has the right to sell the asset
* Strike Price: The price of the asset at the expiration / maturity date

# Arbitration
Arbitrage involves locking in a riskless profit by simultaneously entering into transactions in two or more markets. 

E.g. A certain good costs $X$ USD in US markets and $Y$ SGD in SG markets, but $\$X$ USD converts to $\$(Y-100)$ SGD. If I buy the good in US and sell in SG:
* I have spent $X$ USD = $(Y-100)$ SGD
* I have received $Y$ SGD
* So I have profited by 100 SGD
